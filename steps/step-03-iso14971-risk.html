ISO 14971 risk management

ISO 14971:2019 is the process for managing risk across the whole device lifecycle. It is the most-tested standard in RA interviews because every other activity links back to it. Project folder: docs/regulatory/risk_management_file/.

The five core terms (memorise exactly)

Watch out - the classic confusion
A hazard is not a harm. "Wrong output" is the hazard; "delayed diagnosis" is the harm; the "clinician acting on the wrong output" is the hazardous situation. Interviewers deliberately probe whether you can keep these straight.

The risk management process

  1. Risk management plan - scope, responsibilities, the risk acceptability criteria, the methods.
  2. Risk analysis - identify intended use & reasonably foreseeable misuse; identify hazards; estimate risk (severity x probability).
  3. Risk evaluation - compare each risk against the acceptability criteria.
  4. Risk control - reduce risk using the priority order below; verify each control's effectiveness; check for new risks introduced.
  5. Residual risk evaluation - individual and overall residual risk; overall benefit-risk determination.
  6. Risk management report - conclusion that residual risk is acceptable.
  7. Production & post-production - feed real-world data back in (links to PMS).
Reg - risk control priority (ISO 14971 risk control order)
Controls must be applied in this order, not by convenience:
  1. Inherently safe design (e.g. calibrated confidence, reject out-of-distribution inputs);
  2. Protective measures in the device or manufacturing (e.g. mandatory clinician confirmation, low-confidence warnings);
  3. Information for safety (IFU, labelling, the disclaimer) - the weakest, used last.
You cannot rely on "we put it in the manual" as a primary control.

Hot 2026 - AI-specific hazards
For AI/ML devices, examiners expect risk items that classical software does not have: dataset shift (real-world data drifts from training data), automation bias / over-reliance, out-of-distribution inputs, demographic/Fitzpatrick bias, silent performance degradation, and adversarial / corrupted inputs. The EU AI Act Art. 9 risk-management duties map directly onto ISO 14971 - keep one integrated risk file, do not run two.

ISO 14971 vs the AI Act Art. 9

Both require a continuous, lifecycle risk-management system with identification, estimation, control, and residual-risk evaluation. Best practice (and the easy interview point): run a single integrated risk process that satisfies ISO 14971, MDR, and AI Act Art. 9 together, rather than maintaining parallel files. The accompanying guidance ISO/TR 24971 gives the how-to for 14971.

Interview Q&A

Define hazard, hazardous situation, and harm with an example.

Hazard = a potential source of harm (a wrong AI output). Hazardous situation = exposure to that hazard (a clinician relying on the wrong output). Harm = the actual injury (delayed treatment and disease progression). Risk = probability of that harm times its severity.

In what order must risk controls be applied?

Inherently safe design first; then protective measures in the device or process; then information for safety (labelling/IFU) last. You cannot use the manual as the primary control for a serious risk.

What is residual risk and overall residual risk?

Residual risk is the risk remaining after controls. Overall residual risk is the combined risk of the whole device, which must be judged acceptable against the device's clinical benefit (benefit-risk determination) and documented in the risk management report.

What new risks does AI introduce that traditional software risk analysis misses?

Dataset shift, out-of-distribution inputs, demographic bias, automation bias/over-reliance, lack of explainability, silent degradation over time, and adversarial inputs. These must appear as explicit risk items with controls and post-market monitoring.

How do ISO 14971 and EU AI Act Article 9 relate?

Both mandate a continuous, lifecycle risk-management system. The practical approach is one integrated risk file that demonstrably meets ISO 14971, MDR, and AI Act Art. 9, avoiding duplicate and potentially contradictory records.
