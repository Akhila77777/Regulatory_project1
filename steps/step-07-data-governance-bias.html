Data governance & bias

For an AI device, the data is part of the device. The AI Act dedicates a whole article to it, and bias is the issue most likely to come up in any AI-dermatology interview. Files: docs/data/data_governance.md and docs/data/dataset_card.md.

AI Act Article 10 - what is required

Reg - Art. 10 data & data governance
Training, validation and test datasets must be subject to data-governance practices appropriate to the intended purpose, covering: design choices; data collection and origin; preparation (labelling, cleaning, aggregation); assumptions; assessment of availability/quantity/suitability; examination for possible biases that could affect health/safety or cause discrimination; and measures to detect/prevent/mitigate those biases. Datasets must be relevant, sufficiently representative, and as free of errors and complete as possible for the intended purpose, taking into account the geographical, contextual, behavioural or functional setting.

For each, record in the dataset card: source, licence, size, class distribution, image modality, acquisition devices, and - critically - demographic composition (skin tone, age, sex, geography) and what is missing.

Watch out - the representativeness gap
Public dermoscopy datasets are dominated by light-skinned (Fitzpatrick I-III), mostly European/North-American populations. Models trained on them can under-perform on darker skin (Fitzpatrick IV-VI). This is a documented, well-known limitation - if you do not raise it yourself, an interviewer will, and pretending it does not exist is the worst possible answer.

The Fitzpatrick scale

The Fitzpatrick skin phototype scale (I-VI) classifies skin by its response to UV. It is the standard axis for reporting and stress-testing dermatology-AI fairness. DermaScan should: state its validated Fitzpatrick range in the intended purpose, report per-Fitzpatrick performance, and treat under-represented types as a contraindication / limitation until validated.

Bias: types and mitigations

Bias typeWhat it isMitigation
Selection / samplingTraining data not representative of intended populationSource diverse data; quantify gaps; restrict intended use
Label / measurementGround-truth labels noisy or inconsistentHistopathology-confirmed labels; inter-rater checks
Spurious correlationModel learns artefacts (rulers, ink, hair) not pathologyAugmentation, artefact removal, saliency checks
Automation biasUsers over-trust the outputHuman-oversight design, confidence display, framing
AggregationOne model fits the majority, fails subgroupsSubgroup evaluation; subgroup-aware thresholds
Tip - how to talk about bias well
Do not just say "I would make the data diverse". Show the loop: measure subgroup performance -> document the gap in the dataset card -> add it as an ISO 14971 risk (RISK-XXX) -> control it (limit intended use, warn, collect more data) -> monitor it in PMS. That closed loop is what separates a data scientist from a regulatory-aware AI engineer.

Data protection overlap (GDPR)

Health data is a special category under GDPR Art. 9. Even with public datasets, document the lawful basis, anonymisation/pseudonymisation, data minimisation, and (for any real deployment) a Data Protection Impact Assessment (DPIA). In Germany, also be aware of the BDSG and the new EU Health Data Space (EHDS) for secondary use of health data.

Hot 2026 - data is a compliance artifact
Regulators now treat training data as evidence. Expect to maintain data lineage (where each datum came from, how it was transformed), versioned datasets tied to model versions, and documented bias testing. The European Health Data Space and the AI Act Art. 10 together are pushing "show your data work" from nice-to-have to mandatory.

Interview Q&A

What does AI Act Article 10 require for data?

Documented data-governance over training/validation/test sets: origin, collection, preparation, assumptions, suitability, examination for bias, and mitigation. Data must be relevant, sufficiently representative, and as error-free and complete as possible for the intended purpose and setting.

What is the main bias risk in AI dermatology and how do you handle it?

Under-representation of darker skin tones (Fitzpatrick IV-VI) in public datasets, causing reduced sensitivity for those groups. Handle it by measuring per-Fitzpatrick performance, documenting the gap, restricting the validated intended use, warning in the IFU, logging it as a risk, and addressing it in post-market data collection.

How do you keep data and models traceable?

Version datasets and tie each model version to the exact data and code used to train it, maintain data lineage for transformations, and record splits. This supports reproducibility, change control, and AI Act technical documentation.

How does GDPR interact with an AI medical device?

Health data is special-category data under Article 9, needing a valid condition for processing, minimisation, and usually a DPIA. In Germany the BDSG and the emerging European Health Data Space also apply. Even public datasets need documented lawful basis and anonymisation.
