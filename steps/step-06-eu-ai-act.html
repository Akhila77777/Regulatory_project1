EU AI Act (Regulation 2024/1689)

The world's first comprehensive AI law. It uses a risk-pyramid and bolts onto existing product law like MDR. Knowing how it interacts with MDR is the most marketable thing on this whole site. Folder: docs/regulatory/eu_ai_act/.

The risk pyramid

TierTreatmentExample
UnacceptableProhibited (Art. 5)Social scoring, manipulative AI
High-riskStrict obligations (Art. 8-15 + Annex IV)AI medical devices

Reg - why DermaScan is high-risk (Art. 6(1))
An AI system is high-risk when it is (a) a safety component of, or itself, a product covered by EU harmonisation law in Annex I (which includes the MDR), and (b) that product must undergo third-party conformity assessment under that law. DermaScan is an AI SaMD that is Class IIa under MDR -> needs a Notified Body -> both conditions met -> high-risk. (Annex III lists other standalone high-risk uses, but for devices it is the Art. 6(1) route that bites.)

Tip - the "one file, two laws" message
The smart, senior answer to "isn't this double work with MDR?": the AI Act is designed to dovetail with MDR. You produce a single integrated technical file and a single conformity assessment (the Notified Body assesses both), reusing the ISO 14971 risk system for Art. 9, the clinical/performance data for Art. 15, and the QMS for Art. 17. Article 8(2) explicitly allows integration to avoid duplication.

Roles: provider vs deployer

  • Provider - develops/places the AI system on the market (the manufacturer). Bears the bulk of obligations. DermaScan's "manufacturer" is the provider.
  • Deployer - uses it under their authority (the hospital/clinic). Must ensure human oversight, use it per instructions, monitor.

Also know: QMS (Art. 17), conformity assessment (Art. 43), EU declaration of conformity + CE marking (Art. 47-48), registration (Art. 49), post-market monitoring (Art. 72), serious-incident reporting (Art. 73). These mirror MDR almost one-to-one - that mirroring is the point.

Hot 2026 - the timeline (know the dates cold)
  • 2 Feb 2025 - prohibited practices + AI literacy duties already apply.
  • 2 Aug 2025 - rules for general-purpose AI (GPAI) and governance apply.
  • 2 Aug 2026 - high-risk obligations for standalone Annex III systems apply.
  • 2 Aug 2027 - high-risk AI embedded in regulated products requiring third-party assessment (i.e. AI medical devices) must comply - the extended transition that covers DermaScan.
  • Digital Omnibus (proposed, late 2025/2026) - the Commission has proposed pushing these high-risk dates further (talk of Dec 2027 for standalone and Aug 2028 for embedded), largely because the harmonized standards are not ready yet. Treat dates as moving and say so.
Watch out - missing standards
A key reason for the proposed delays is that the harmonized standards and common specifications needed to operationalise Art. 9-15 for AI are still under development by CEN-CENELEC. Until they exist there is no clean "presumption of conformity" route for AI - so manufacturers work from drafts, ISO/IEC 42001, and best practice. Knowing this nuance separates you from candidates who only memorised the article numbers.

Annex IV - the AI technical documentation

Annex IV is the contents list for the high-risk technical file: general description & intended purpose; detailed system description and development process; the data and data-governance description; the human-oversight measures; the metrics for accuracy, robustness and cybersecurity; the risk-management system; and the post-market monitoring plan. This is exactly the structure to use in docs/regulatory/eu_ai_act/.

Interview Q&A

Why is an AI medical device classed as high-risk under the AI Act?

Under Article 6(1), because it is a safety component of (or is) a product covered by EU harmonisation law (the MDR) and that product requires third-party conformity assessment. An MDR Class IIa+ AI SaMD meets both conditions.

List the core high-risk obligations.

Risk management (Art. 9), data governance (10), technical documentation (11), logging/record-keeping (12), transparency to deployers (13), human oversight (14), and accuracy/robustness/cybersecurity (15), plus a QMS (17), conformity assessment (43), CE marking (48), and post-market monitoring and incident reporting (72-73).

Does the AI Act mean double the work alongside MDR?

No - it is built to integrate. Article 8(2) lets you merge the documentation and assessment so the Notified Body assesses both at once. You reuse the ISO 14971 risk system, clinical/performance evidence, and QMS, adding AI-specific elements (data governance, oversight, robustness, logging).

When does the AI Act apply to AI medical devices?

High-risk obligations for AI embedded in MDR/IVDR devices needing a Notified Body apply from 2 August 2027 (standalone Annex III systems from 2 August 2026). The Digital Omnibus has proposed pushing these later, partly because the harmonized standards are still being developed, so the dates are in flux.

What is the difference between a provider and a deployer?

The provider develops and places the AI system on the market and holds most obligations; the deployer uses it under its authority (e.g. a hospital) and must ensure human oversight, follow the instructions, and monitor performance.
