Intended purpose & SaMD

The intended purpose (also "intended use") is the anchor of the entire regulatory file. Classification, risk, requirements, clinical evidence, and post-market plans are all derived from it. Project file: docs/regulatory/intended_use.md.

Is it even a medical device? (qualification)

Under MDR Article 2(1), a device is an instrument, software, etc. intended by the manufacturer to be used for a medical purpose such as diagnosis, prevention, monitoring, prediction, prognosis, treatment, or alleviation of disease. Note: it is the manufacturer's stated intent that decides qualification, not the technology.

Reg - what makes software a medical device (SaMD)
MDCG 2019-11 gives the qualification logic. Software is a medical device when it performs an action on data beyond storage, archival, communication, or simple search and does so for a medical purpose for the benefit of an individual patient. DermaScan analyses an image to flag malignancy risk for a specific patient -> it qualifies. The international term is SaMD (Software as a Medical Device), defined by IMDRF as software intended for a medical purpose that performs that purpose without being part of a hardware medical device.

SaMD vs SiMD

  • SaMD - standalone software that is itself the device (DermaScan, a diagnosis-support app).
  • SiMD - Software in a Medical Device, i.e. embedded firmware driving hardware (e.g. control software in an infusion pump).

What an intended-purpose statement must contain

ElementFor DermaScan (example, conservative)
Medical purposeDecision support for triage of pigmented skin lesions
IndicationAdult patients with a pigmented skin lesion of concern
Intended userQualified clinician (dermatologist / GP), not the patient
Use environmentClinical setting, dermoscopic or standardised clinical imaging
InputA single dermoscopic/clinical image meeting stated quality criteria
OutputA malignancy-risk probability with calibrated confidence + mandatory disclaimer
What it is NOTNot a diagnosis; does not replace histopathology or clinical judgement
Contraindications / limitsNon-pigmented lesions, poor-quality images, image types/skin tones outside the validated range
Patient population & demographicsState the validated Fitzpatrick range; flag any under-represented groups
Watch out - scope creep
The most common (and most penalised) mistake is overclaiming. "Detects skin cancer" implies a diagnostic device making a definitive call. "Assists a qualified clinician in triaging pigmented lesions" is a decision-support tool with human oversight. The wording you choose directly changes your classification, your risk profile, and your liability. Always write the narrowest honest claim.
Tip - intended purpose drives everything
In an interview, if asked "where do you start with a new product?", the right answer is always: "With the intended purpose, because it determines qualification, classification, the applicable GSPRs, the risk analysis scope, and the clinical evidence I need."

Intended purpose vs the EU AI Act

The AI Act also leans on intended purpose: a system's "intended purpose" (Art. 3) plus its status as a safety component of a regulated device determines whether it is high-risk. Keep the MDR intended purpose and the AI Act intended purpose identical and consistent - inconsistency between the two is an easy audit finding.

Interview Q&A

How do you decide whether a piece of software is a medical device?

Look at the manufacturer's intended purpose against MDR Article 2 and apply the MDCG 2019-11 logic: does it do more than store/transmit/search, and does it act on data for a medical purpose benefiting an individual patient? If yes, it qualifies. The technology is irrelevant; the claimed intended use is decisive.

What is the difference between SaMD and SiMD?

SaMD is standalone software that is itself the medical device and achieves its purpose without being part of hardware. SiMD is software embedded in and controlling a hardware device. DermaScan is SaMD.

Why is the intended-purpose statement so important?

It is the single source of truth: it determines qualification, classification (and therefore the conformity route), which GSPRs apply, the scope of the risk file, the clinical evidence needed, the IFU content, and the post-market plan. Change the intended purpose and you must revisit all of those.

How would you phrase DermaScan's intended purpose defensively?

As a decision-support tool intended to assist qualified clinicians in triaging pigmented skin lesions by providing a malignancy-risk estimate, with mandatory human review, explicitly not a diagnosis and not a replacement for histopathology, validated for a stated population and image type.
