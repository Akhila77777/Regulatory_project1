The RA landscape in Germany

Before any standard, understand the playing field: what Regulatory Affairs does day to day, the institutions, and where the 2026 demand is. This is what lets you talk like an insider in interviews.

What does a Regulatory Affairs professional do?

RA is the function that gets a medical device legally onto the market and keeps it there. Core activities:

  • Classification & strategy - decide the regulatory class and the route to market (which annex, which Notified Body).
  • Technical documentation - compile and maintain the technical file (MDR Annex II/III) that proves conformity.
  • Standards mapping - show how the product meets the General Safety and Performance Requirements (GSPR) via harmonized standards.
  • Liaison - interface with Notified Bodies and competent authorities (in Germany, BfArM and the Lander authorities).
  • Lifecycle maintenance - manage changes, post-market surveillance, vigilance reporting, and renewals.

RA overlaps heavily with Quality Assurance (QA/QMS) and Clinical Affairs. Many early roles are titled "RA/QA Specialist". The user's background (Test Engineer / Quality Specialist) is a natural bridge into this.

Reg - MDCG guidance
The Medical Device Coordination Group (MDCG) publishes the practical guidance documents everyone actually uses (e.g. MDCG 2019-11 on software qualification, MDCG 2019-16 on cybersecurity). Cite MDCG numbers in interviews - it signals you have read the real material.

Who hires, and for what

  • Manufacturers: Siemens Healthineers, B. Braun, Drager, Fresenius, Brainlab, plus a deep Mittelstand of mid-size device makers and digital-health startups.
  • Notified Bodies & test houses: TUV SUD, TUV Rheinland, DEKRA - they hire reviewers/auditors.
  • Consultancies & CROs: regulatory and clinical service providers.
  • Authorities/research: BfArM, universities (thesis route, e.g. the Johner Institute ecosystem).
Hot 2026 - the market
Regulatory Affairs specialists with MDR + IVDR + EUDAMED + AI Act depth are described as the single most contested profile in European MedTech. Dual-fluency (regulatory + AI, or regulatory + software/supply-chain) commands a premium. Emerging job titles: AI Governance Officer, Regulatory Automation Engineer, Digital Therapeutics Specialist. Roughly two-thirds of open technical roles in German MedTech cluster around software integration, RA technologists, and healthcare data scientists.

Interview Q&A

What does a Regulatory Affairs department actually do?

It owns the legal path to market and the lifecycle of compliance: classification and regulatory strategy, compiling and maintaining technical documentation, demonstrating conformity to the GSPR through standards, liaising with Notified Bodies and competent authorities, and managing post-market obligations (PMS, vigilance, change control). It works closely with QA, R&D, and Clinical.

What is the difference between a Notified Body and a competent authority?

A Notified Body is a private, accredited organisation designated to perform conformity assessment and issue CE certificates (e.g. TUV SUD). A competent authority is the government regulator that oversees the market and the Notified Bodies, handles vigilance and market surveillance - in Germany that is BfArM (federal) together with the Lander authorities and the ZLG that designates NBs.

What is BfArM responsible for?

It is Germany's federal institute for drugs and medical devices: vigilance and incident handling, market surveillance support, and notably the DiGA fast-track directory for reimbursable digital health applications.

Why are RA + AI profiles in such demand in 2026?

Because four regulatory streams (MDR, IVDR, EUDAMED, EU AI Act) hit overlapping deadlines while AI features flood into devices, and very few people can speak both the regulatory and the AI/data-science language. That dual-fluency is exactly what DermaScan demonstrates.
